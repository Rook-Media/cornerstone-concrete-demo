What Is It

Commercial Concrete Flatwork

Commercial flatwork refers to any horizontal concrete surface at or near grade — parking lots, warehouse floors, loading dock aprons, equipment pads, exterior plaza slabs, and site concrete. Unlike residential flatwork, commercial flatwork must be engineered for load, traffic, and operational requirements that can be orders of magnitude greater than a residential driveway.

Proper commercial flatwork starts long before the concrete truck arrives. Subbase preparation is the single largest factor in long-term performance — a poorly compacted or poorly graded base will produce a failing slab regardless of concrete quality. We evaluate bearing capacity, specify base depth and material, and verify compaction before we form a single foot of slab edge.

Reinforcement design, joint layout, mix design, and finishing specifications all follow from the intended use. A forklift-rated warehouse floor has fundamentally different requirements than a retail parking lot, and we spec each project accordingly. We work from structural drawings when provided, and can develop project specifications from scratch when engineering drawings aren't available.

Control joint placement is one of the most commonly mishandled elements in commercial flatwork. Incorrect joint spacing and depth are the leading cause of uncontrolled cracking in commercial slabs. Our crews follow engineering-specified joint layouts — or develop them based on ACI guidelines — and cut joints at the correct time in the curing process to ensure cracks form where intended.

Common Questions

Commercial Flatwork FAQ

What flatness tolerances can you achieve on warehouse floors?+
We can achieve F-numbers (FF and FL ratings) specified by your racking contractor or engineer. Superflat floors for VNA (very narrow aisle) equipment require specialized equipment and screeding techniques that our crew is trained for. We discuss flatness requirements during the pre-construction phase so the right equipment and process is in place before we pour.
How do you determine the right concrete thickness?+
Slab thickness is determined by the expected load conditions — vehicle type and weight, axle loads, racking loads transmitted through columns, and fork truck capacity. We reference your structural drawings when available. When drawings are not provided, we use ACI 330 (parking lots) or ACI 360 (industrial floors) guidelines to develop the specification for your specific use case.
What is the minimum cure time before forklift traffic?+
As a general guideline, concrete reaches approximately 70% of its 28-day design strength at 7 days. For standard forklift traffic, we typically require a minimum of 7 days. For heavy equipment or heavily loaded forklifts, we may extend that recommendation. Concrete strength is also weather-dependent — we factor ambient temperature and the mix's curing characteristics into our specific recommendation for your pour.
Can you work around an active facility?+
Yes. We regularly phase commercial flatwork projects to allow facilities to continue operating during construction. We coordinate pour sequences, temporary traffic control, and handoff points with your operations team to minimize disruption. Weekend and overnight work is available for critical phases where downtime is not acceptable.
Do you provide break test results and QC documentation?+
Yes. We coordinate with third-party testing labs for cylinder break tests when required by spec or owner. All QC documentation — break reports, pour logs, mix design records, and flatness measurements — is compiled and delivered at project close. This documentation is available for building permit closeout, owner records, or future reference.
